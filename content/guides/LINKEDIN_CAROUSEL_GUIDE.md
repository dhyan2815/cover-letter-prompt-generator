# How To Create A High-Performing Carousel
**By Kamini Pramanik, Content Designer**

To understand the potential of a well-crafted carousel, the guide highlights examples of successful posts achieving massive reach, such as 206,974 impressions, 85,101 impressions, and 37,335 impressions. Successful example topics include "Best color combinations", "Top 01% Creators NEVER do these 5 Branding Mistakes", and "How to Create A LinkedIn Banner that Converts".

Below is the step-by-step framework to achieve these results, including visual design guidelines for your slides.

---

### Step 01: Hook Like A Pro
Your first slide decides everything. If your hook doesn't stop the scroll, nothing else matters.
* Make it bold, clear, and emotion-driven.
* Use numbers or strong words.
* Keep it short - under 10 words.

**Visual Design Implementation:** The visual examples demonstrate high-impact, high-contrast title slides. One example uses a vibrant purple background with large, stylized typography reading "How to Create Your CUSTOM GPT IN CHATGPT," accompanied by a clean image of a laptop screen. Another uses an eye-catching, high-quality image of an AI-generated Santa Claus with bold, capitalized text stating "HOW TO MAKE A PERSONALISED AI SANTA VIDEO". The focus is entirely on the main subject and the text.

### Step 02: Build A Story Flow
Don't just share tips - take them on a journey. Stories make learning memorable.
* Start with a problem.
* Agitate the pain.
* Lead to the solution.
* End with a takeaway.

**Visual Design Implementation:** The guide illustrates this progression visually. Slide one sets the topic, such as "How to generate branded pitch decks (using an Al Agent)". The next slide establishes authority ("I've built and lead creative teams..."). The following slide introduces the core friction using bold text: "Our biggest problem: Building 100s of presentation decks". The subsequent slides then provide the solution in clear, actionable steps, like "Step 1 Go to gamma.app and sign up (free)↓".

### Step 03: Keep It Skimmable
Write for fast readers, not deep readers. People don't read. They glance.
* 1 idea per slide.
* Short lines, big spacing.
* Use emojis or icons for rhythm.

**Visual Design Implementation:** The document shows a stark contrast between a bad and a good slide. The "bad" slide is filled with dense, edge-to-edge paragraphs of small text explaining font inconsistencies, which is visually exhausting. The "good" slide completely removes paragraphs, replacing them with a checklist format featuring short, punchy directives like "Go to chatgpt.com," "Go to the GPTs store," and "Click Start Chat" alongside corresponding icons. 

### Step 04: Use High-Quality Images
Low-quality visuals kill credibility. Quality builds trust.
* Sharp, relevant images only.
* No stretched or pixelated stock photos.
* Consistent style across slides.

**Visual Design Implementation:** The examples feature incredibly crisp, professional, and visually striking imagery. One slide uses a highly detailed, futuristic image of a human head with an exposed, glowing brain and a YouTube logo to support the text "How To Create YOUTUBE THUMBNAILS LIKE TOP INFLUENCERS". Another utilizes a polished 3D graphic of a fragmented head containing multiple colorful faces to illustrate "CHATGPT HAS 8 PERSONALITIES". The visuals are thematic and flawless.

### Step 05: Design That Guides
Your visuals should lead the eye. Design isn't decoration - it's direction.
* Use arrows, lines, shapes for direction.
* Keep spacing balanced.
* Highlight keywords intentionally.

**Visual Design Implementation:** A "SETUP" slide demonstrates this by housing instructional text inside neat, numbered, pill-shaped boxes (1, 2, 3), forcing the reader's eye to process the steps sequentially (e.g., "Add a YouTube thumbnail for reference," "Add your prompt"). Another example uses simple bullet points with generous spacing between questions like "Where does your company stand?" and "Which way are budgets trending?" to ensure each point is digested individually.

### Step 06: Visual Hierarchy
Make it easy to follow what matters most. Your reader should know where to look - instantly.
* Big headline → smaller subtext.
* Contrast important words.
* Don't overload visuals.

**Visual Design Implementation:** To show what *not* to do, an example displays scattered text boxes of similar sizes ("BRAND NEW FASHION," "BLACK FRIDAY," "70% OFF," "SHOP NOW") placed haphazardly, creating confusion about where to look first. The correct example establishes clear dominance: the main hook "70% OFF" is massive and bold, the category "ALL FASHION ITEMS" is a secondary size, and the call to action "SHOP NOW!" is clearly separated as a distinct button, with minor details placed in the smallest font. 

### Step 07: Prioritize Readability
Make every words readable. If they can't read it, they'll scroll past it.
* Use clean fonts.
* Keep text big.
* Limit to 1-2 font styles.
* High contrast between background and text.

**Visual Design Implementation:** The guide provides a direct visual test regarding color contrast. A "Pass" visual shows stark black text ("Aa") on a yellow background, providing excellent legibility with a Contrast Ratio of 15.99:1. A "Fail" visual places light orange text ("Aa") on the exact same yellow background, making the text incredibly difficult to read and resulting in a poor Contrast Ratio of 1.09:1.

### Step 08: Stick To Clean Layouts
More white space = more focus. Your design should feel calm, not crowded.
* Avoid clutter.
* Let elements breathe.
* Keep one message per slide.

**Visual Design Implementation:** An example slide titled "6 Rules to Find Your Creator-Audience Fit" perfects this rule by leaving the majority of the slide completely blank. In the center, it utilizes a highly simplified Venn diagram showing the overlap between "Creator" and "Audience" to equal "Fit". There are no background patterns, no unnecessary icons, and the text is kept to an absolute minimum.

### Step 09: Call to Action (CTA) and End Screens
*(Note: The text for Step 09 repeats the principles of Step 08: More white space = more focus. Avoid clutter. Let elements breathe. Keep one message per slide. Your design should feel calm, not crowded. However, the visuals provided focus heavily on the final CTA).*

**Visual Design Implementation:** The final slides of your carousel should cleanly drive engagement. The examples show slides with singular, bold requests. One slide features large text asking, "FOUND THIS USEFUL? SAVE & REPOST". Another promotes following the creator: "Follow me to build FAST WITH AI". A third variation offers a list of specific actions: "Like this post? Repost if you found this insightful. Save to refer back to these terms later.". 

**Final Reminder:** Always remember to ask your audience if they "Found it helpful?" and encourage them to "Repost" and "Follow for more content".
